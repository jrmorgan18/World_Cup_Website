## Playing Style and Outlook

Germany are ranked this high because the talent is undeniable, but they are still carrying the weight of the last two World Cups. Back-to-back group-stage exits in 2018 and 2022 seem almost inconceivable for Germany. This is a country that used to treat the quarterfinals as a birthright. Now the first job is to look like Germany again.

The good news is that Julian Nagelsmann does seem to have built something with a clearer identity. This is not the slower, more rigid possession team that got stuck in bad habits near the end of the Joachim Löw era. The current version are more vertical, explosive and difficult to defend, usually working from a 4-2-3-1 or 4-3-3 with Wirtz and Musiala operating between the lines, runners providing width, and Kai Havertz or Nick Woltemade acting as the advanced reference point.

That attacking group is what makes this version of Germany a real contender. Musiala and Wirtz can both play as central creators, both can drift wide, both can combine in tight spaces, and both can force defenders to make uncomfortable choices. If opponents sit deep, Germany have the technical quality to play through them. If opponents press, Germany have enough ball-carriers and quick passers to break forward into space.

The 6-0 win over Slovakia in the final qualifier showed the best version. Germany scored four in the first half, Wirtz assisted Sané twice, Woltemade continued his national-team breakthrough, and Slovakia had no answer for the tempo and pressure. That result matters because it was not just a comfortable win over a weak opponent. It was a pressure game, with automatic qualification on the line, and Germany handled it like a serious team.

There are still questions. The goalkeeper situation changed late, with Manuel Neuer reversing his international retirement and being named Nagelsmann's No. 1 for the tournament. On one hand, that gives Germany a 2014 World Cup winner and one of the great goalkeepers ever. On the other, it is a pretty dramatic pivot after Oliver Baumann had started all six qualifiers and looked set to own the job. Neuer's return raises the ceiling if he is sharp, but it also adds a little tension to a team trying to move into a new era.

The defense is better than it has been in recent tournaments, but it is not completely airtight. Nico Schlotterbeck, Jonathan Tah, Antonio Rüdiger and Malick Thiaw give Nagelsmann real options at center back. Kimmich gives control if he plays right back. David Raum gives width and aggression on the left. The vulnerability is what happens when Germany's attacking players and fullbacks all push at once, preventing opponents from exploiting the space in behind.

The group is favorable, and Germany should win it. Curaçao are a great story, but Germany should treat that opener as a chance to settle the nerves and build goal difference. Ivory Coast are the more dangerous pure-athletic matchup, with enough pace and power to punish a sloppy German buildup. Ecuador may be the toughest tactical opponent, compact, disciplined, strong in midfield and comfortable making a favorite work for everything.

That final match against Ecuador could be more important than the betting odds suggest. If Germany have six points already, they may be managing minutes and thinking about the knockout bracket. If they slip against Ivory Coast, suddenly Ecuador becomes a real test of whether this team has actually matured. Germany's draw gives them a runway, but it also gives them opponents who can expose the exact transition issues that have hurt them before.

The ceiling is obvious. A team with Musiala, Wirtz, Kimmich, Havertz, Sané, Woltemade, Pavlović, Schlotterbeck, Rüdiger and Neuer has enough talent to reach a semifinal. But Germany are not quite in the top inner circle of contenders, mostly because the scars from 2018 and 2022 are still too fresh and the defensive balance is not fully proven. They are clearly good enough to punish the bracket if the attack clicks. The question is whether this is a real German revival or just another talented team that looks good on paper until the tournament gets uncomfortable.
