Declan Rice is not the most exciting name in England's squad, which says more about England than it does about Rice. Jude Bellingham has the superstar aura, Harry Kane is still the captain and scorer, and Arsenal teammate Bukayo Saka is the flashy winger. But Rice is the heart of the team and the player that sets the foundation for the rest of the talent around him.

He comes into the World Cup in his prime, still only 27, already established as one of the best midfielders in Europe, the centerpiece of an Arsenal team on the brink of a Premier League title and valued by Transfermarkt at $141.1m. That is a huge number for a midfielder whose value is not built around highlight goals or viral dribbles, but it fits the player he has become at Arsenal and England, a ball-winner, organizer, carrier, set-piece threat, and increasingly a midfielder who can influence games higher up the pitch.

For England, Rice is the balancing piece. Thomas Tuchel has to figure out how to fit together Bellingham, Phil Foden, Cole Palmer, Bukayo Saka, Kane, and the rest of that attacking group without leaving the midfield exposed. Rice is the player who makes those conversations possible. He can sit as the No. 6 if England want more attackers on the field, or play as a more aggressive No. 8 if Tuchel wants another holding midfielder next to him. Either way, his job is basically to make sure England don't become too top-heavy.

He also matters more in attack than his reputation sometimes suggests. Rice led England in qualifying assists with four, during a perfect campaign in which England went 8-0-0, scored 22 goals and conceded none. That is a good reminder that he is not just a destroyer. His delivery on set pieces, switches of play, and ability to carry through midfield can be real weapons, especially in tournament games where England may need to break down compact teams.

England's ceiling probably depends on the attackers, but their floor depends on him. If Rice is controlling space, winning duels, and giving Bellingham and the front line a platform, England can look like one of the few teams with both elite talent and real structure. If he gets isolated or has to cover too much ground, the whole thing can start to look like a collection of stars rather than a team.

A few England players may generate more headlines. Rice may end up being the one who decides whether those headlines are about another near miss or the tournament where England finally turn all this talent into something real.
