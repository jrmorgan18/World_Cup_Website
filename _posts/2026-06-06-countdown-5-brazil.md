**How they Qualified:** Finished fifth in CONMEBOL qualifying to take one of South America's direct spots. It was not a vintage Brazil campaign: they went 8-4-6 with a plus-7 goal difference, a major drop from the dominant 2018 and 2022 qualifying cycles.

**Best World Cup Finish:** Champions (1958, 1962, 1970, 1994, 2002). Brazil are still the only five-time World Cup winners and are chasing their first title in 24 years.

**Key Players:**
- Vinícius Júnior — Winger — Real Madrid (Spain). Vinícius is the player who gives Brazil their clearest path to looking like Brazil again. Transfermarkt values him at $176.4m, the highest mark in the squad, and under Carlo Ancelotti the plan should be pretty simple: give him structure behind the ball, then isolate him on the left and let him terrorize fullbacks. If Vinícius is merely good, Brazil are a contender. If he is the best attacker in the tournament, they can win it.
- Raphinha — Winger — Barcelona (Spain). Raphinha is not as famous as Vinícius or Neymar, but he is the player who gives Brazil the most complete attacking balance. Transfermarkt values him at $94.1m and he has been a vital cog in Barcelona's back to back La Liga title campaigns. He gives Brazil end product, defensive work, set-piece quality and a right-sided threat that keeps the attack from becoming too tilted toward Vinícius.
- Gabriel — Center Back — Arsenal (Premier League). Gabriel may be the most important non-attacker in the squad. Transfermarkt values him at $88.2m, and he gives Brazil a left-footed center back who can defend the box, win aerial duels, play out under pressure and attack set pieces. He has been the centerpiece of Arsenal's top defense in Europe this season, leading them to the Premier League title and Champions League final. His pairing with Marquinhos is central to the more balanced Ancelotti version of Brazil.

## Playing Style and Outlook

Brazil are still Brazil, but they do not enter this World Cup with the same invincible glow that has surrounded them in the past. In some sense they've yet to regain their world-beating aura ever since the embarrassing 7-1 loss to Germany on home soil in the 2014 World Cup. Since winning the trophy in 2002, Brazil have exited in the quarterfinals in four of the last five World Cups, with the 2014 semifinal humiliation the lone exception and its own kind of trauma. The pattern has been familiar. Dazzling pieces, big expectations, then one knockout game where the attack stalls, the structure cracks, or the emotional weight becomes too heavy.

The talent is still enormous, the shirt still carries its own gravity, and the betting market rightly has them in the inner circle of contenders. But the last two decades have turned Brazil into merely one of the contenders instead of the perennial favorite.

That is why Carlo Ancelotti matters so much. Brazil did not hire him to make them more Brazilian in the romantic sense. They hired him to make them more practical. His track record is impeccable, the only manager to win titles in all five of the top European leagues, with a record-setting five Champions League titles.

This version of Brazil is likely to be more measured, more balanced and more comfortable winning in multiple ways than some of the recent Brazil teams that looked spectacular until the first truly uncomfortable knockout game. They generally set up in a 4-3-3 or 4-2-3-1, with patient buildup designed to release the front three into better attacking zones rather than forcing the game too early.

The attacking structure starts with Vinícius on the left and Raphinha on the right. That gives Brazil two very different wide threats. Vinícius is the chaos agent. The winger who can wreck a fullback and bend the whole defensive shape toward him. Raphinha is more complete, bringing pressing, combinations, set piece delivery, and the intelligence to attack the back post in the right moments. That balance gives Brazil an attack that still functions when the opponent sits deep, fouls, doubles Vinícius and dares someone else to solve the game.

The central attacking role is less settled. Rodrygo and Estêvão are both out injured, which removes two of the more natural high-end attacking options. João Pedro was left out. The central-forward competition looks to be between Matheus Cunha, Endrick, Neymar and Igor Thiago, with each offering something different. Cunha gives mobility and combination play. Endrick gives youthful explosiveness and finishing upside. Igor Thiago gives a more traditional striker profile. Neymar gives creativity, set pieces and aura, but also the biggest medical and tactical question on the roster.

Neymar is a subplot that will follow Brazil everywhere. Ancelotti recalled him after more than two years away from the national team, and Neymar said he felt physically strong after working back from serious injuries. However, he suffered a grade-two calf injury in late May, was ruled out of Brazil's final friendlies, and is likely to miss the World Cup opener against Morocco. That probably makes the cleanest version of Brazil one where Neymar is a situational weapon rather than the central focus. That is probably for the better, as Brazil need Vinícius to be the lead attacker, Raphinha to provide the other-side threat, and Neymar to add creativity in carefully managed moments.

The midfield is another sign that Ancelotti wants control and protection. Casemiro is back in the picture and gives Brazil a familiar defensive anchor. Bruno Guimarães gives them ball progression, bite and the ability to play through pressure. Lucas Paquetá, Danilo and Fabinho give him different blends of control, legs and defensive security. This midfield demonstrates the new more pragmatic approach. They may not bring the "beautiful game" flair that Brazil is known for, but they can win duels, protect the center backs, and get the ball to the attackers in positions where they can actually hurt people.

Defensively, Brazil may be sturdier than they have been in years. Gabriel and Marquinhos give them a serious center-back pairing. The issue is still the space around them. If the fullbacks push high and the wingers stay aggressive, Brazil can leave pockets in transition. That is where Ancelotti's pragmatism matters. This team cannot afford to defend like every attack will end in a goal. It has to be compact enough to survive the moments when it loses the ball.

The group is favorable, but it gives Brazil a real test right away. Morocco are the kind of opponent that can make favorites miserable. They are compact, experienced, athletic, dangerous in transition, and not remotely intimidated after reaching the 2022 semifinals. The opener at MetLife forces Brazil to be serious immediately. If they beat Morocco, the group should open up. If they draw, they are still fine. If they lose, the old anxiety arrives before the tournament has even settled in.

The match with Haiti may be one of the most lopsided of the tournament, and may be an opportunity for Ancelotti to manage minutes, test the center-forward hierarchy, and build attacking rhythm. Scotland are more awkward than glamorous. They will be physical, organized, direct and dangerous on set pieces. Brazil should have too much talent, but Scotland are the kind of underdog that can turn a group finale into a grind if Brazil get impatient.

Brazil should win Group C. The market has them heavily favored to win the group and almost automatic to advance. Winning the group will not satisfy any Brazilian fans, nor will simply winning a few knockout games. Brazil will be judged by how they compete against the other elite sides in the latter rounds of the tournament.

Ancelotti is here to break the pattern of early departures. Brazil do not need to be the most fun team in the tournament. They need to be the most adaptable. The ceiling is the trophy. The concern is that we have said some version of that before. This time, the difference has to be structure. Brazil still have the magic. Ancelotti's job is to make sure they finally have the platform for it to matter.
