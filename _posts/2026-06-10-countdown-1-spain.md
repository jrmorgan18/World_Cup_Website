**Group:** H — Spain, Cape Verde, Saudi Arabia, Uruguay

**How they Qualified:** Won UEFA Group E. Spain officially qualified after a 2-2 draw with Turkey, finishing unbeaten at the top of the group. They won their first five qualifiers by a combined 19-0 before finally conceding in that last match, and they stretched their unbeaten run to 31 matches in the process.

**Best World Cup Finish:** Champions (2010). Spain are making their 17th World Cup appearance and have now qualified for every tournament since 1978.

**Transfermarkt Roster Value:** $1.43 B

**FIFA Rank:** 2

**Odds to Win Group:** -475

**Odds to Advance:** -10000

**Odds to Win Cup:** +450

**Key Players:**
- Lamine Yamal — Winger — Barcelona (Spain). Yamal is the reason Spain feel different from every recent version of Spain. They have always had midfielders. They have always had control. They have not always had a wide attacker who can turn that control into terror. Transfermarkt values him at $235.2m, making him Spain's highest-valued player, and even that somehow undersells how central he already feels to the whole project. He was one of the breakout stars of Euro 2024, became the youngest scorer in European Championship history, and now arrives at the World Cup as the player who can make Spain both the best possession team in the tournament and one of the most dangerous attacking teams. The only concern is fitness, with Spain taking a cautious approach after recent hamstring and groin issues, but Luis de la Fuente has sounded optimistic that he will be ready for the opener.
- Pedri — Midfielder — Barcelona (Spain). Pedri is the player who makes Spain feel like Spain again. Transfermarkt values him at $176.4m, and his importance is not just that he is brilliant, but that his brilliance is exactly the kind Spain are built around. He receives in tight spaces, plays one-touch combinations, breaks pressure, finds the pass before the pass, and gives Spain the kind of midfield rhythm that most countries simply cannot replicate. If Yamal is the lightning, Pedri is the current running through the whole team.
- Rodri — Midfielder — Manchester City (Premier League). Rodri's value is listed at $58.8m, which is almost comically low relative to his importance when healthy, but that is mostly because his recent injury history has changed the market math. On the field, there may not be a more important midfielder in world soccer. Spain can play without him because their midfield depth is absurd, with Martín Zubimendi, Fabián Ruiz, Mikel Merino, Gavi and others all capable of filling major roles, but the highest version of Spain still has Rodri giving them structure, defensive cover and big-game calm at the base of midfield. He is the player who turns beautiful possession into safe possession.

## Playing Style and Outlook

Spain come in at number one because they are the cleanest team in the tournament. France may have more overwhelming attacking depth. England may be deeper in certain areas. Argentina may still have the champion's belief. But Spain are the team where the pieces make the most sense together.

This is not just a vibes pick based on old Spain mythology. This is a team that won Euro 2024, then rolled through World Cup qualifying, then arrived here with a midfield group that looks like it was designed in a lab to suffocate tournament games. Spain are no longer just a possession team that passes the ball around and waits for someone to solve the final third, an issue that plagued some of the post-2012 versions of Spain. This version has the control and the thrust.

The biggest reason is Yamal. He changes the whole geometry of Spain's attack. When Spain had Xavi, Iniesta, Busquets and David Villa, they could dominate through the middle and still find a cutting edge. The later versions often had the domination without quite enough fear factor. Yamal restores that fear factor. He can receive wide, hold width, beat the first defender, slip a pass through the channel, or come inside and score. He is the kind of player who forces a team that wants to stay compact to stretch just a little wider than it wants, and that is all Spain need. Give Pedri, Rodri, Zubimendi, Olmo, Ruiz or Merino an extra yard in midfield, and the whole structure starts to crack.

That is why Spain are so difficult to play against. You cannot simply sit deep and concede the ball, because they have enough creators to move you around until the gap appears. You cannot press recklessly, because their midfield is the most press-resistant unit in the tournament, and you cannot overcommit to Yamal, because Nico Williams, Dani Olmo, Ferran Torres and Mikel Oyarzabal give them enough variety from the other side and through the middle to punish that imbalance.

The midfield is the biggest separator between Spain and France. France have the bigger names in attack and maybe the more frightening center backs, but Spain have arguably the best midfield in the world. A healthy Rodri gives them a Ballon d'Or level number six. Pedri gives them a midfielder who can control the ball in impossible spaces. Zubimendi would start for almost anyone else. Fabián Ruiz was excellent at Euro 2024. Mikel Merino gives them a more vertical, physical option. Gavi gives them edge, running and nastiness. Dani Olmo can slide between midfield and attack. It is not just depth. It is depth with different tools.

That matters because World Cups are often won by teams that can solve different types of games. Spain can win the game where they have 65% of the ball and patiently break down Cape Verde or Saudi Arabia. They can also win the game where Uruguay turn it into a fight, because they now have more speed, more size and more bite than the prettiest versions of Spain. They are not a team of 11 small technicians anymore. Pau Cubarsí gives them a young, elite passing center back. Robin Le Normand gives them physical defending. Marc Cucurella, Alejandro Grimaldo and Pedro Porro give them different fullback profiles. At goalkeeper they have a wealth of riches with possibly the two best goalies in Europe this season, David Raya of Arsenal and Joan Garcia of Barcelona, likely backing up entrenched starter Unai Simón. The squad is balanced in a way that makes the ranking feel less like a bet on one superstar and more like a bet on the whole machine.

The only concern for Spain is whether they have enough true penalty-box certainty at striker. Álvaro Morata has aged out of this version of the team, and Spain are now relying on combinations of Oyarzabal, Ferran Torres, Olmo, Yamal, Nico Williams and others to supply the goals. That can work, and in some ways it makes them harder to defend because the front line is fluid. But in a tight quarterfinal or semifinal, when the opponent has survived the first 70 minutes and the game starts to feel heavy, Spain may still want one ruthless No. 9 who turns one half-chance into a goal. That is the one thing France have with Mbappé, Norway have with Haaland, England have with Kane, and Argentina has with Lautaro or Julian Alvarez.

The other concern is transition defense. Spain's best version keeps the ball so well that it prevents counters before they happen, but the World Cup field is full of teams built to punish one bad turnover. France can do it. Brazil can do it. England can do it. Portugal can do it. Even Uruguay in the group stage can do it with Federico Valverde, Darwin Núñez and a Bielsa-style willingness to make the game uncomfortable. That final group match is a decent test because Uruguay are the kind of opponent that can make a beautiful team prove it also has teeth.

However, the group draw is favorable enough that anything short of first place would be a disappointment. That is one other reason Spain edge France out for the top of the list. While France will have to fight it out with one of the best teams from Africa in Senegal, and a Norway led by Erling Haaland, Spain's path to the top of the group is much easier.

Cape Verde are a great story as a first-time World Cup team, but Spain should handle them without breaking much of a sweat. Saudi Arabia can be organized and dangerous in moments, but Spain should control that match as well. Uruguay are the real opponent, and that game could shape Spain's knockout path. Win the group, and Spain likely enter the knockouts with momentum and the kind of draw a tournament favorite expects. Finish second, and things can get much more difficult much faster.

The broader question is whether Spain's Euro 2024 rise was the start of a new cycle or the peak of it. I lean strongly toward the former. The core is almost absurdly well-timed. Yamal is still a teenager. Pedri is still young enough that it feels impossible he has already been around this long. Cubarsí is just getting started. Nico Williams is in his prime runway. Zubimendi, Olmo, Ruiz and Merino are in the middle of their careers. Rodri, if fit, is still the adult in the room. This is not an old team trying to squeeze out one last tournament. It is a team that already won a major tournament and somehow still looks like it has another level to reach.

That is what makes Spain the pick at number one. They are not perfect, but they are the team with the fewest structural questions. France need the midfield to glue together. England need the talent to translate into tournament control. Brazil need consistency. Argentina need time to keep being kind. Spain already have the clearest identity, the best midfield, one of the most dangerous young attackers in the world, and a manager who has already shown he can turn all of that into a trophy.

Spain can win this World Cup because they can dictate the terms of almost any match. They can slow you down, move you around, press you into mistakes, and then suddenly have Yamal or Nico Williams turn the whole thing vertical. They are still recognizably Spain, but no longer trapped by the old idea of Spain. This version has the passing. It has the control. It has the edge. And for the first time in a long time, it has the winger who can make all that control feel terrifying.
