**Group:** L — England, Croatia, Ghana, Panama

**How they Qualified:** Won UEFA Group K. England went eight wins from eight, and Reuters noted that they did not concede a single goal in qualifying. Harry Kane scored twice in the final qualifier, a 2-0 win away to Albania.

**Best World Cup Finish:** Champions (1966)

**Transfermarkt Roster Value:** $1.54 B

**FIFA Rank:** 4

**Odds to Win Group:** -280

**Odds to Advance:** -10000

**Odds to Win Cup:** 7-1

**Key Players:**
- Harry Kane — Striker — Bayern Munich (Germany). Kane is still the player England are built around. He is England's talisman and entering the tournament as the country's record scorer with 78 goals in 112 appearances, coming off a third straight Bundesliga scoring title with 36 league goals. Transfermarkt values him at $70.6m, but the real value is tactical: he is England's finisher, penalty taker, captain, and one of their best creators when he drops off the front line.
- Jude Bellingham — Midfielder — Real Madrid (Spain). Bellingham is England's highest-valued player at $164.6m, and probably the player with the highest ceiling in the squad. The tactical question is exactly where he plays. Whether it's as a deeper midfielder, roaming No. 10, or something in between is one of the unresolved issues in Tuchel's England. But if Bellingham is used correctly, he gives England ball-carrying, box arrivals, pressing, edge, and a level of big-game personality few midfielders in the tournament can match.
- Declan Rice — Midfielder — Arsenal (Premier League). Rice is the balancing piece. Transfermarkt values him at $141.1m, and he may be the player who decides whether England can fit all the attacking talent together without becoming too open. He can sit, carry, win second balls, defend transitions, and take some of the set-piece responsibility. If Kane and Bellingham are the headline pieces, Rice is the one who makes the whole structure feel less like a collection of names and more like a team.

## Playing Style and Outlook

England are the most talented team in the tournament that still somehow feel unsettled. The squad value is enormous, the qualifying record was perfect, and the top-end talent is obvious. The question with England has rarely been whether they have enough good players. It is whether they can turn those players into a clear tournament identity before the pressure arrives.

Thomas Tuchel has already made it clear he is not just picking the 26 most famous names. Phil Foden, Cole Palmer and Trent Alexander-Arnold were all left out of the final squad, while Ivan Toney earned a surprise recall and Kobbie Mainoo made it in. Tuchel then framed the decision around chemistry, admitting the squad might lack the "wow factor" of some previous England groups but saying the priority was building the right collective.

That is the right idea, but it also raises the stakes. If you leave out Foden and Palmer, the team has to look coherent. England cannot have the usual post-tournament conversation where everyone wonders why the manager could not fit the best attackers together. Tuchel has made the choice of structure over star-stacking, defined roles over pure talent accumulation. Now the team has to justify it.

The base shape is likely to live somewhere between a 4-2-3-1 and a 4-3-3, but the real shape depends on Bellingham. If Bellingham is the No. 10, Rice probably needs a cleaner partner next to him so England do not get stretched. If Bellingham plays as a No. 8, England can use Morgan Rogers, Eberechi Eze, Anthony Gordon or another attacker in more advanced spaces. If Tuchel wants more control, he can lean into Rice, Mainoo and Bellingham. If he wants more running and width, Bukayo Saka becomes even more crucial on the right.

Kane is the other tactical key. England are at their best when Kane's dropping movement creates lanes for runners. That can mean Saka attacking from the right, Gordon running beyond from the left, Bellingham arriving through the middle, or Eze/Rogers combining between the lines. But there is always a danger with Kane that England become too drawn toward him. If he drops and nobody runs beyond, the attack slows down. If he stays high and England cannot connect midfield to attack, they can look blunt.

That dependence was visible in the spring, when Kane missed the March friendly defeat to Japan through injury and England looked badly short of a reference point without him. It is not just the goals. It is the leadership, the passing, the calm, and the way he gives everyone else a reference point. Ollie Watkins and Ivan Toney are useful alternatives, but neither replicates the full Kane package.

The encouraging part is that England's defensive base should be real. Eight qualifying matches, eight wins, zero goals conceded is a significant accomplishment, even if the group competition was weak by European standards. Jordan Pickford is tournament-tested. Marc Guéhi, John Stones, Ezri Konsa, Jarell Quansah and Dan Burn give Tuchel different center-back profiles. Reece James, Tino Livramento, Djed Spence and Nico O'Reilly give him more fullback variety than England have had in a while. The defense may not have one obvious, locked-in back four, but it has options.

The group gives England a serious test right away. Croatia are not as fresh as they were in 2018, but they still have Luka Modrić, tournament muscle memory, and a long history of making more talented teams uncomfortable. Opening against Croatia is not a gentle warm-up, it is a direct test of whether England can control a veteran team that knows how to slow games down, survive pressure and make opponents impatient. Croatia also carry the obvious history from the 2018 semifinal, when they beat England 2-1 after extra time.

Ghana are the wild-card match. They made a late coaching change, bringing in Carlos Queiroz after firing Otto Addo. They have Antoine Semenyo, Thomas Partey, Iñaki Williams, Jordan Ayew and enough athleticism to make England defend transitions seriously. They also have goalkeeper questions and limited time under Queiroz, which makes them dangerous but hard to trust.

Panama are the match England should win, but they are not quite the same novelty side England beat 6-1 in 2018. Panama have risen under Thomas Christiansen and are aiming for their first World Cup match win after strong regional results, including a Nations League runner-up finish and Copa América quarterfinal appearance. England should have too much, but if they enter the final group match needing a result, Panama will be organized enough to make it more difficult than the rankings suggest.

The ceiling is obvious: England can win the World Cup. Kane, Bellingham, Rice, Saka, Pickford and that defensive pool are good enough to beat anyone. They have the squad value, the qualifying record, the manager, and the recent tournament experience. Since 2018, England have been to a World Cup semifinal, a Euros final, a World Cup quarterfinal and another Euros final. This is no longer a team hoping to learn how to go deep. They have been close enough that the only satisfying ending left is winning.

The concern is that this still feels slightly unfinished. Tuchel has made bold squad decisions. Bellingham's role is not fully settled. Kane dependency remains real. Saka's fitness and rhythm matter. The left side needs the right balance. The center-back pairing has options but not one obvious answer. Those are not fatal flaws, but they are the kinds of questions that become very loud in a semifinal against Spain, France, Argentina or Brazil.

That is why England land at No. 3. They are not the safest team in the field, and they may not be the most coherent yet. But they have as much winning upside as anyone. If Tuchel's chemistry bet pays off, if Kane is healthy, if Bellingham finds his best role, and if Rice holds the whole thing together, England are absolutely capable of ending the 60-year wait.
