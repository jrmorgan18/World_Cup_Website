Bruno Fernandes might be Portugal's most important player, even in a squad where Cristiano Ronaldo still dominates the storyline and Vitinha may be the midfielder everyone suddenly agrees is world-class. Ronaldo is the legend, Vitinha is the controller, Bernardo Silva is the smooth connector, but Bruno is the player who most directly turns Portugal's possession into chances, shots, and goals.

He comes into the World Cup off a strong club season. Fernandes was named the 2025-26 Premier League Player of the Season after leading Manchester United to a third-place finish, breaking the league's single-season assist record with 21, scoring nine goals, and leading the Premier League with 136 chances created, almost double the next best player, Dominik Szoboszlai with 78. That is exactly the kind of production Portugal need from him to make a serious run at their first World Cup trophy.

Bruno Fernandes is comfortable taking risks. He tries the difficult pass. He can look frantic compared with a calmer midfielder like Vitinha. But that is also the point. Portugal have enough players who can circulate the ball and keep things neat. Vitinha and João Neves are excellent at cleaning up mistakes behind him, enabling Fernandes to operate with freedom. Fernandes is the one willing to force the issue before a defense gets comfortable, and in a World Cup knockout game, that kind of aggression can be the difference between sterile control and an actual breakthrough.

His role also becomes fascinating because of Ronaldo. Portugal's squad announcement was framed around Ronaldo going to a sixth World Cup at age 41, but Roberto Martínez's team is much deeper than one legend chasing one last trophy. Martínez named a squad loaded with attacking options, including Ronaldo, João Félix, Bernardo Silva and Rafael Leão, with Portugal drawn into Group K against DR Congo, Uzbekistan and Colombia. In that kind of team, Bruno's job is partly tactical and partly emotional, feed Ronaldo when the moment is there, but also make sure Portugal do not become too focused on servicing one player.

That is why this may be the perfect Bruno Fernandes tournament. He is 31, still in his prime, old enough to be one of the leaders, but not old enough to be fading. He is surrounded by more midfield control than he has with Manchester United, which should free him to do what he does best and drive the attack. He can provide danger in multiple ways, picking a defense apart with a pass, providing precise set pieces, or arriving late in the box.

Portugal are good enough to win the World Cup, but they have been talented before. Martínez said recently that this expanded tournament will be chaotic and that talent alone will not be enough. Bruno is one of the players who can help them navigate that chaos. If Portugal finally turn all this talent into a world title, it will likely be because Fernandes created enough game-breaking moments to push them over the top.
