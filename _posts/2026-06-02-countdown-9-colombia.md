**Group:** K — Portugal, Colombia, DR Congo, Uzbekistan

**How they Qualified:** Finished third in CONMEBOL qualifying. Colombia clinched their place with a 3-0 win over Bolivia, then closed qualifying with a wild 6-3 win at Venezuela, finishing 7-7-4 with 28 goals scored.

**Best World Cup Finish:** Quarterfinals (2014)

**Transfermarkt Roster Value:** $350.9 M

**FIFA Rank:** 13

**Odds to Win Group:** +240

**Odds to Advance:** -1000

**Odds to Win Cup:** 40-1

**Key Players:**
- Luis Díaz — Winger — Bayern Munich (Germany). Díaz is the obvious headliner and the player who gives Colombia their real tournament ceiling. Transfermarkt values him at $82.3m, the highest mark in the squad, and he was Colombia's top scorer in qualifying with seven goals. He is the one Colombian attacker who can consistently tilt the field by himself, whether that means carrying the ball in transition, pressing fullbacks into mistakes, or creating a chance when the rest of the attack has gone stale.
- James Rodríguez — Attacking Midfielder — Minnesota United (MLS). James is not the same player who owned the 2014 World Cup, but he is still the creative brain of this team. He was Colombia's qualifying assist leader with seven, and he is their corner, free-kick and penalty option. The concern is rhythm. He is 34 now, his club situation has been uneven, and there are fitness questions after a difficult start in MLS. But if James is sharp, Colombia's attack looks much more connected.
- Daniel Muñoz — Right Back — Crystal Palace (Premier League). Muñoz is one of the players who makes Colombia more than just Díaz and James. Valued at $31.8m, he gives Lorenzo a right back who can defend with edge, attack aggressively, and provide the fullback width that is central to Colombia's shape. In a 4-2-3-1 where Díaz wants to come inside from the left and James wants to drift into pockets, Muñoz's ability to stretch the right side is a major part of the balance.

## Playing Style and Outlook

Colombia represents the first real dark horse with an outside chance of winning the whole thing. They have a real star in Díaz, a beloved tournament icon in James, a physical back line, dangerous fullbacks, and enough attacking options to make them feel like a team nobody will want to see in a knockout game. They also have just enough wastefulness and defensive chaos to make you wonder whether the whole thing can unravel.

Under Néstor Lorenzo, the base shape is usually a 4-2-3-1, with a double pivot behind James and a front line built around Díaz's left-sided threat. Colombia is a possession team with set-piece quality and a physical back line, built around Díaz-led transitions, James as the creative hub and width from the fullbacks. Colombia want the ball, but they are probably at their most dangerous when they can turn a recovery into a fast attack before the opponent gets set.

The qualifying campaign showed why the optimism is real. Colombia finished third in South America, ahead of Uruguay, Brazil and Paraguay on goal difference, and scored 28 goals, behind only Argentina in the final table. The 6-3 win over Venezuela in the final qualifier captured the fun version, with Luis Suárez scoring four times and Lorenzo praising the team for always trying to be the protagonist. That attacking ambition is part of their identity now. They are not a passive counterpuncher. They want to impose themselves.

But the March friendlies added a necessary warning label. Colombia lost 2-1 to Croatia and then 3-1 to France, which raised real questions about whether Colombia can hang with top European teams. Top among them are whether Lorenzo has found the right center-forward answer and whether James can still be the connector at this level.

That striker question is probably the biggest tactical issue. Jhon Córdoba scored the goal that helped clinch qualification against Bolivia. Luis Suárez scored four against Venezuela. Rafael Borré is still around. But none of them feels like a fully settled World Cup No. 9. Díaz can create danger on the left, Muñoz can get forward on the right, and James can still deliver the final ball. The issue is whether all of that turns into enough clean finishing.

The group is favorable enough that Colombia should advance, but not so favorable that they can relax. Uzbekistan are tournament debutants, but they are organized and will treat the opener like the biggest match in their history. DR Congo have pace and set-piece threat, and their match with Colombia in Guadalajara could be more uncomfortable than the rankings suggest. Portugal are the clear group favorite and have the deepest squad, but that final match in Miami could feel close to a Colombia home game with the crowd behind them.

Colombia are good enough to push Portugal, good enough to win the group if the opener goes smoothly, and dangerous enough that a second-place finish could still lead to a real knockout run.

The ceiling is probably a quarterfinal or semifinal push if Díaz catches fire, James stays healthy enough to create, and the striker spot gives them something reliable. The floor is a frustrating exit where Colombia control games, miss chances, and get punished by one bad defensive stretch. That volatility is exactly why they belong this high. Colombia are not one of the safest teams in the tournament, but they might be one of the most dangerous.
