**Group:** H — Spain, Uruguay, Saudi Arabia, Cape Verde

**How they Qualified:** Finished fourth in CONMEBOL qualifying to take one of South America's direct spots. Uruguay sealed qualification in September, returning to the World Cup for a fifth straight tournament.

**Best World Cup Finish:** Champions (1930, 1950)

**Transfermarkt Roster Value:** $426.2 M

**FIFA Rank:** 17

**Odds to Win Group:** +370

**Odds to Advance:** -1000

**Odds to Win Cup:** 65-1

**Key Players:**
- Federico Valverde — Midfielder — Real Madrid (Spain). Valverde is the engine of the whole team and Uruguay's most valuable player at $141.1m. He gives Marcelo Bielsa almost everything he wants in one player: pressing, range, ball-carrying, defensive recovery, long shooting, and enough versatility to adjust within matches. If Uruguay are going to make this more than a respectable group-stage showing, Valverde has to be one of the best midfielders in the tournament.
- Darwin Núñez — Striker — Al-Hilal (Saudi Arabia). Núñez remains one of the great chaos players in international soccer. Transfermarkt values him at $29.4m, but his real value is harder to measure because he changes the way opponents have to defend. He stretches back lines, attacks space, presses aggressively, and creates chances that do not seem to exist. His finishing can be streaky, but Uruguay need him to turn all that activity into goals.
- Ronald Araújo — Center Back — Barcelona (Spain). Araújo gives Uruguay the defensive profile they need against the better teams in this group. Transfermarkt values him at $23.5m, and while injuries have sometimes interrupted his rhythm, his combination of size, speed, and one-on-one defending is still vital. Against Spain especially, Uruguay will need a center back who can defend space, survive isolation, and keep the game from turning into a long passing exercise around their box.

## Playing Style and Outlook

Uruguay are one of the most fascinating teams in the tournament because the talent is real, the coach is famous, the history is enormous, and the volatility is obvious. Under Marcelo Bielsa, they are not trying to be the old, low-block Uruguay that simply suffered, fouled, and waited for a set piece. This version wants to run, press, attack vertically, and make the game uncomfortable through speed and pressure. The current version is a high-intensity team built around vertical transitions, physical pressing, and set-piece threat. That is exactly the Bielsa idea.

The midfield is the reason the plan can work. Valverde is the centerpiece, but Manuel Ugarte matters almost as much because he gives them ball-winning and bite in front of the defense. If those two are covering ground and winning second balls, Uruguay can look like a nightmare. If the spacing gets stretched, though, they can look very open very quickly.

That is the core tension with this team. Uruguay have enough talent to beat almost anyone in the field on the right day, but they are not always controlled. The 5-1 friendly loss to the United States in November was the warning sign. It was only a friendly, but Bielsa himself did not brush it off, saying a defeat like that generates concern and that the project would continue as planned through the World Cup.

The group is not terrible, but it isn't a complete cake walk. Saudi Arabia are the opener, and Uruguay really need three points there. The Saudis are dangerous enough in transition to make that uncomfortable, but Uruguay should have too much midfield power and attacking quality. Cape Verde are one of the best stories of the tournament, but Uruguay should still see that as a match they are expected to win. If they take six points from those two games, the final match against Spain becomes a shot at the group.

That Spain match is the real measuring stick. Spain are the clear favorites in Group H, and the market prices them that way. But Uruguay are the one team in the group with enough athleticism and midfield quality to make Spain uncomfortable. If Valverde and Ugarte can turn that match into a running game instead of a possession drill, Uruguay can make it interesting. If Spain can settle into rhythm, Uruguay may spend too much time chasing.

The knockout path is the bigger concern. Uruguay are good enough to advance, and the odds reflect that. But finishing second behind Spain could mean a much tougher Round of 32 draw than a team of this quality would want. That is why the Saudi Arabia opener matters so much. Uruguay cannot afford a slow start and then hope to solve everything against Spain.

The ceiling is a quarterfinal-level team, maybe even a little higher if Núñez gets hot and Valverde plays like one of the best midfielders in the world. The floor is messier — a talented but stretched team that gets punished for Bielsa's aggression. That makes Uruguay dangerous, entertaining, and a little hard to trust, which is probably exactly where they belong in this countdown.
